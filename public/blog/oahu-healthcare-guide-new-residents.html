O'ahu Healthcare Guide for New Residents: Doctors, Hospitals, Insurance & What to Set Up Before You Land

Healthcare is the thing most people forget to research before moving to Hawaii — until they need it and realize they have no idea how it works here. This guide covers everything: the best hospitals, how insurance works differently in Hawaii, how to find a doctor, and what to set up before your move so you're never caught without coverage or care.

How Healthcare Works Differently in Hawaii

Hawaii has one of the most distinctive healthcare systems in the United States — and if you're moving from the mainland, there are a few things that will surprise you.

The Hawaii Prepaid Health Care Act

Hawaii is the only state with a law requiring most employers to provide health insurance to employees who work 20 or more hours per week. This has been in place since 1974 and means that employer-sponsored coverage is more broadly available here than almost anywhere in the country. If you're taking a job in Hawaii, your employer almost certainly provides health insurance.

If you're self-employed, a freelancer, or running your own business, you'll need to find your own coverage. The Hawaii Health Connector (Hawaii's ACA marketplace) is your starting point, or you can buy directly from HMSA or Kaiser.

The Two Dominant Insurers

The Hawaii health insurance market is largely a two-horse race: HMSA (Hawaii Medical Service Association — the state's Blue Cross Blue Shield affiliate) and Kaiser Permanente Hawaii. Between them, they cover the vast majority of Hawaii residents. You will encounter these names constantly.

Understanding the difference matters for how you access care:

  • HMSA is a traditional insurance plan — you choose your own doctors from their network, get referrals when needed, and pay claims through the insurer. They have the broadest network on O'ahu.
  • Kaiser is a closed system — you see Kaiser doctors at Kaiser facilities, everything is integrated. It's efficient and often less expensive, but you can't go outside the Kaiser system for non-emergency care without significant out-of-pocket cost.

If you value flexibility and having access to the widest range of specialists, HMSA is usually the better choice. If you value convenience, integrated records, and lower costs, Kaiser is excellent — provided you're within range of their facilities.

Access vs. Cost

Healthcare costs in Hawaii are broadly comparable to major mainland metros. The bigger adjustment is access. O'ahu has a finite number of physicians for a population of about one million, and some specialties are genuinely undersupplied. New patient wait times of 4–8 weeks for primary care are common. For specialists like rheumatology, endocrinology, or certain surgical subspecialties, waits can stretch to months.

The solution: set up your healthcare before you need it, not after.

The Best Hospitals on O'ahu

O'ahu has several hospitals serving different parts of the island. Here's what you need to know about each:

The Queen's Medical Center — Honolulu

Queen's is the island's flagship hospital and the only Level I Trauma Center in Hawaii (and across the Pacific). If you have a life-threatening emergency anywhere on O'ahu, you'll likely end up at Queen's. It has the broadest range of specialists, the most advanced surgical capabilities, and the highest volume of complex cases on the island. Queen's is affiliated with major mainland academic medical centers and regularly recruits physician talent from those systems.

Best for: Emergencies, complex surgery, oncology, cardiology, neurology

Location: Downtown Honolulu — most accessible from urban Honolulu, Manoa, Makiki, and Punchbowl

Straub Medical Center — Downtown Honolulu

Straub is the go-to for outpatient specialty care for many Honolulu residents. It has a strong multispecialty physician group (Straub Clinic) and excellent outpatient imaging, labs, and procedure capabilities. Their behavioral health program is among the better options on island.

Best for: Outpatient specialty care, primary care, behavioral health, orthopedics

Location: Downtown Honolulu, near Ala Moana

Kapiolani Medical Center for Women & Children — Honolulu

If you're moving to O'ahu with young children or are pregnant, Kapiolani is where you want to be. It's the state's only dedicated women's and children's hospital, handles the vast majority of births in Hawaii, and has the island's best NICU and pediatric specialists.

Best for: Obstetrics, pediatrics, NICU, neonatal care

Location: Honolulu, near Kaimuki/Diamond Head

Pali Momi Medical Center — Aiea

Pali Momi serves central O'ahu — Pearl City, Aiea, Ewa Beach, and Kapolei residents. It's a full-service community hospital with a good emergency department and solid outpatient services. Not as large as Queen's or Straub, but often has shorter wait times for non-critical care.

Best for: Central/West O'ahu residents, general emergencies, outpatient care

Location: Aiea — convenient for Pearl City, 'Aiea, Ewa, Kapolei

Castle Medical Center — Kailua

Castle is the primary hospital serving the Windward side of O'ahu — Kailua, Kaneohe, and surrounding communities. If you're living in Kailua or Lanikai, Castle is your closest full-service hospital. It's a well-regarded community hospital with a good emergency department, though complex cases are typically transferred to Queen's.

Best for: Windward O'ahu residents, general care, routine surgeries

Location: Kailua

Health Insurance in Hawaii: HMSA vs. Kaiser vs. Your Mainland Plan

If You're Employed in Hawaii

Your employer will almost certainly offer HMSA, Kaiser, or both. During open enrollment or your new hire period, compare carefully:

  • If you have children, check which plan includes Kapiolani providers
  • If you have established specialists you want to continue seeing, check HMSA's network
  • If cost and convenience are your priority, Kaiser's all-in model often wins on monthly premium

If You're Self-Employed or Remote

Visit hawaiihealthconnector.com (Hawaii's ACA exchange) or go direct to HMSA (hmsa.com) or Kaiser (kp.org/hi). Both offer individual and family plans at various metal tiers. Most self-employed people on O'ahu go with HMSA for the flexibility.

Can You Keep Your Mainland Plan?

Technically yes, if your plan has national network coverage. In practice, most mainland-based plans have limited in-network providers in Hawaii. You'll pay out-of-network rates for most care, which gets expensive fast. The exception: if your employer is a large national company with a truly national network plan (some BCBS national plans, for example), you may find HMSA-affiliated providers in-network. Call your insurer directly and ask: "Which providers are in-network in Honolulu, Hawaii?"

Our strong recommendation: if you're staying in Hawaii more than 6 months, switch to a Hawaii-based plan. The savings and simplicity are worth it.

How to Find a Primary Care Doctor on O'ahu

This is the most important healthcare step when you move, and the one most people put off too long.

Start With Your Insurance Network

HMSA's provider search at hmsa.com is solid — filter by location (enter your O'ahu zip code), specialty (Primary Care or Family Medicine or Internal Medicine), and "Accepting New Patients." Kaiser members go through Kaiser's own system; your PCP will be assigned at a Kaiser facility near you.

Book Before You Move

This is the single most useful thing you can do. Most O'ahu PCPs have 4–8 week waits for new patients. If you call the week you land, your first appointment might be two months out. If you book before you leave the mainland, you'll have an appointment in your first few weeks on island.

Good Networks to Know

  • Straub Clinic — large multispecialty group, excellent for Honolulu residents (straub.net)
  • Hawaii Pacific Health — system that includes Queen's, Pali Momi, Kapiolani, and Wilcox (hawaiipacifichealth.org)
  • Kaiser Permanente Hawaii — integrated system, excellent if you commit to it fully (kp.org/hi)
  • Ohana Pacific Health — smaller independent group, good for Windward and North Shore communities

Telehealth as a Bridge

In your first weeks, before you have a PCP established, telehealth services like Teladoc, MDLive, or your insurer's telehealth portal can handle minor issues, prescription refills, and urgent non-emergency needs. Most HMSA and Kaiser plans include telehealth at low or no cost.

Specialist Access on O'ahu: What's Available and What Isn't

O'ahu has good specialist coverage for common needs, with some notable gaps. Here's the honest picture:

Well-Covered Specialties

  • Cardiology — Queen's Heart Center is world-class; Straub and Pali Momi also have strong cardiology
  • Oncology — The Queen's Cancer Center is the island's flagship; Hawaii Cancer Care (private) also has strong options
  • Orthopedics — Multiple strong practices; active population drives good orthopedic supply
  • OB/GYN — Excellent, driven by Kapiolani's strength
  • Dermatology — Good supply (sun, humidity, and a health-conscious population help)

More Limited Specialties

  • Rheumatology — Limited practitioners; waits can be 3–6 months for new patients
  • Endocrinology — Similar story; book early if you need an endocrinologist
  • Psychiatry — Significant shortage; telepsychiatry is often the practical solution
  • Certain surgical subspecialties — Complex cases (some neurosurgery, transplant, etc.) may require travel to mainland medical centers

If you rely on a specialist for a chronic condition, research your O'ahu options before you move — not after you arrive and discover there's a 5-month wait.

Mental Health Resources on O'ahu

Mental health care on O'ahu is a known access challenge, and it's worth being honest about it. There are good options — they just require more planning than on the mainland.

In-Person Therapy

Psychology Today's therapist directory (psychologytoday.com/us/therapists/hi/honolulu) filtered to Honolulu gives you a good starting list. Filter by insurance and specialty. The Straub Clinic's behavioral health department and Kaiser's mental health program are solid institutional options. Private practice therapists exist across the island, with concentration in Honolulu and Kailua.

Teletherapy (Often the Best First Option)

For many new residents, teletherapy is the fastest and most practical route to mental health care. Services like BetterHelp, Talkspace, or mainland-licensed therapists offering telehealth visits work well — and some mainland therapists are licensed in Hawaii and can continue treating you after your move. Ask your current therapist if they hold a Hawaii license.

Psychiatry

If you need psychiatric care (medication management), book early. The Queen's Medical Center, Straub, and Kaiser all have psychiatric services, but new patient waits for psychiatry are often 2–4 months. If you're on psychiatric medication, get a 90-day supply and a clear prescription before your move.

Crisis Resources

  • Crisis Line of Hawaii: (808) 832-3100 (24/7)
  • National 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline: Call or text 988
  • Queen's Emergency Department: (808) 691-4400

Urgent Care & After-Hours Options by Neighborhood

For non-emergency situations that need same-day attention, O'ahu has a solid network of urgent care clinics:

  • Honolulu / Ala Moana: Straub Urgent Care, Kaiser Moanalua
  • Kailua / Windward: Castle Urgent Care (Kailua), Urgent Care Hawaii (Kailua)
  • Pearl City / Aiea: Pali Momi Urgent Care, Kaiser Pearl City
  • Kapolei / Ewa: Kaiser Moanalua West, Urgent Care Hawaii (Kapolei)
  • North Shore: Kahuku Medical Center (small but full-service emergency)
  • Hawaii Kai: Kaiser Hawaii Kai, Straub Hawaii Kai clinic

For true emergencies, don't go to urgent care — go directly to Queen's Medical Center or call 911. Urgent care is for things like minor injuries, infections, UTIs, flu symptoms, and situations that need same-day attention but aren't life-threatening.

Pre-Move Healthcare Checklist

Do these before your moving truck leaves:

  • ☐ Request copies of all medical records from every current provider — PCP, specialists, dentist, eye doctor
  • ☐ Get 90-day supplies of all medications — insurance transitions and new pharmacy setups cause gaps
  • ☐ Ask your therapist or psychiatrist if they're licensed in Hawaii (many will do telehealth across state lines)
  • ☐ Research Hawaii insurance options — HMSA vs. Kaiser vs. your current plan for Hawaii coverage
  • ☐ Find your O'ahu PCP before you leave — book the new patient appointment now, not after you land
  • ☐ If you have children: identify a Kapiolani-affiliated pediatrician and book a first appointment
  • ☐ If you rely on a specialist: research their O'ahu equivalent and book an intro call or appointment
  • ☐ Save key numbers in your phone: Crisis Line of Hawaii, nearest urgent care, Castle or Queen's ED (depending on your neighborhood)
  • ☐ If you're pregnant or planning to be: contact Kapiolani OB immediately — their practices fill up

Frequently Asked Questions

Is healthcare expensive in Hawaii compared to the mainland?

Hawaii has unique healthcare economics. The Hawaii Prepaid Health Care Act requires most employers to provide health insurance to employees working 20+ hours/week, making coverage broadly available. Out-of-pocket costs are comparable to major mainland metros. The bigger challenge is access — O'ahu has limited specialists in some fields, and wait times can be longer than you're used to.

What are the best hospitals on O'ahu?

The Queen's Medical Center (Honolulu, Level I Trauma Center) is the island's flagship. Straub Medical Center excels in outpatient specialty care. Kapiolani is the best for obstetrics and pediatrics. Pali Momi serves central O'ahu well. Castle Medical Center is the go-to for Windward residents.

Can I keep my mainland health insurance when I move to Hawaii?

Technically yes, but practically difficult. Most mainland plans have limited in-network providers in Hawaii. Most new residents find it easier and less expensive to switch to HMSA or Kaiser. Check your plan's Hawaii network coverage before assuming you're covered.

How do I find a primary care doctor in Hawaii?

Use HMSA's provider search or Kaiser's directory, filter by your area and "accepting new patients," and book before you move. Most PCPs have 4–8 week waits for new patients. Call the Hawaii Medical Association at (808) 536-7702 for referrals if you're stuck.

What should I set up before moving to O'ahu health-wise?

Get 90-day medication supplies, request all medical records, research Hawaii insurance options, book a PCP new patient appointment before you leave, and identify O'ahu equivalents for any specialists you rely on. The checklist in this article covers everything.

Is mental health care accessible on O'ahu?

It's improving but limited. Teletherapy is often the fastest option in your first months. In-person options exist through Kaiser, Straub, and private practice therapists. Psychiatry has the longest waits (2–4 months) — book early and secure a 90-day medication supply before you move.
