O'ahu Utility Setup Guide for New Residents: HECO, BWS, Gas, Internet, and More

Setting up utilities in Hawaii is different from anywhere on the mainland — different providers, different pricing, and a few things that don't exist here at all. Here's exactly what to do, in what order, before you arrive.

Electricity — Hawaiian Electric (HECO)

There is one electricity provider on O'ahu: Hawaiian Electric (HECO). No competition, no choice. HECO serves the entire island.

How to Set Up Service

  • Online: hawaiianelectric.com → Start/Stop/Transfer Service
  • Phone: 808-548-7311 (Mon–Fri 7:30am–4:30pm HST)
  • What you need: New address, move-in date, SSN or ITIN (credit check), valid photo ID, and a deposit if you have no prior HECO account history (typically $130–$250)

Timing

HECO can typically activate service within 1–2 business days. Set up your account at least 5 business days before move-in to avoid any gap. If you're transferring service from another HECO address, the process is faster — same-day or next-day in most cases.

Hawaii's Electricity Rates

Hawaii consistently ranks #1 in the US for residential electricity costs — currently approximately $0.38–$0.45 per kilowatt-hour, compared to the US average of $0.12–$0.15/kWh. This is not a typo. Running your air conditioning on O'ahu is expensive. Your electricity bill will be significantly higher than your mainland bill for the same size home.

What this means practically:

  • A 3-bedroom O'ahu home with A/C: $200–$400/month in electricity
  • A 2-bedroom condo, moderate A/C use: $120–$220/month
  • A 4-bedroom home with frequent A/C and a pool pump: $350–$550/month

Mitigation strategies: ceiling fans, cross-ventilation (Hawaii's trade winds make many homes livable without A/C for much of the year), solar panels, and LED lighting throughout.

Budget Billing and Payment Options

HECO offers a budget billing program that averages your annual electricity usage into equal monthly payments — helpful for budgeting. Auto-pay and paperless billing are available. If you're in a condo, verify whether electricity is included in your HOA fees (some older Honolulu condos bundle utilities) or separately metered.

Water — Board of Water Supply (BWS)

O'ahu's water is provided by the Honolulu Board of Water Supply, a semi-autonomous city agency. O'ahu has excellent water quality — the island's aquifer system produces some of the best-tasting tap water in the US, and most residents drink it without filtering.

How to Set Up Service

  • Online: boardofwatersupply.com → Customer Services → Start Service
  • Phone: 808-748-5000 (Mon–Fri 7:45am–4:30pm HST)
  • What you need: Service address, move-in date, photo ID, security deposit ($50–$100 for most residential accounts)

What You're Actually Doing

In most cases, water is already flowing to the property — you're transferring billing into your name, not requesting a new connection. If the property has been vacant, BWS may need to do a meter check, which takes 1–3 business days. Set up BWS service the same week as HECO.

Water Rates and Bills

BWS rates are tiered based on usage. Most 3-bedroom households pay $45–$85/month. Water bills on O'ahu are significantly lower than mainland cities in California and the Pacific Northwest. Sewer charges are billed separately by the city and are typically $30–$60/month for residential accounts.

Irrigation Water

For homes with large yards or irrigation systems (common in East Honolulu and Windward O'ahu), check whether the property has a separate irrigation meter. Irrigation accounts have different rates and billing. Your property manager or prior owner should be able to confirm.

Gas on O'ahu — What You Need to Know

This surprises many mainland movers: O'ahu does not have a natural gas distribution network in most areas. No pipeline running to your home like you had in California, Texas, or the Pacific Northwest.

What O'ahu Has Instead

  • Hawaii Gas (The Gas Company): Provides propane (LPG) delivery by truck to homes with propane tanks, and synthetic natural gas (SNG) via utility lines in certain Honolulu neighborhoods (primarily downtown and parts of East Honolulu). If your new home has a gas range or water heater, it's likely propane or SNG — check with your landlord or prior owner.
  • Propane tanks: Common for homes with gas appliances outside the SNG service area. Tank ownership and delivery are coordinated through Hawaii Gas (808-535-5933) or independent propane suppliers.
  • No gas at all: The majority of O'ahu condos and newer homes use electric appliances exclusively. If you're coming from a gas-heavy kitchen, be prepared to adjust — induction cooktops are increasingly popular in Hawaii for this reason.

Hawaii Gas Service Areas

Hawaii Gas utility service (SNG via lines) is available in parts of Honolulu proper. Outside this area — Kailua, Ko Olina, North Shore, Hawaii Kai, Aina Haina, Manoa — most homes are all-electric or propane. Verify before you move what fuel your new home uses for cooking, water heating, and any other gas appliances.

Internet Service Providers on O'ahu

O'ahu has decent internet infrastructure by island standards, with fiber and cable available across most residential areas. The main providers:

Spectrum (Cable)

The most widely available provider on O'ahu. Cable internet with speeds up to 1 Gbps in most urban areas. Available in Honolulu, Waikiki, Pearl City, Ewa Beach, and most suburban neighborhoods. Not always available in rural North Shore or parts of Windward O'ahu. Typical residential plan: $50–$80/month for 300–500 Mbps. No contracts on most plans.

To check availability and schedule installation: spectrum.com or 833-267-6094. Installation takes 5–14 days; book before you arrive.

Hawaiian Telcom (Fiber / DSL)

Hawaii's local telecom provider offers fiber-to-the-home service in an expanding footprint across O'ahu. Where fiber is available, it's excellent — symmetrical speeds up to 1 Gbps. DSL is also available where fiber isn't yet deployed, though speeds are slower. Typical residential plan: $60–$100/month for fiber.

To check availability: hawaiiantel.com or 808-643-3456.

Starlink (Satellite)

SpaceX Starlink is a game-changer for O'ahu residents in areas with limited cable/fiber availability — particularly North Shore, Windward O'ahu, and rural areas. Speeds of 50–200 Mbps, latency around 20–40ms (significantly better than traditional satellite). Equipment cost ~$350–$599 (one-time), service $120/month. Order at starlink.com; equipment ships directly, self-install.

Mobile Hotspot / 5G

T-Mobile and Verizon both have strong 5G coverage across O'ahu's urban areas. For renters waiting for internet installation, or residents in areas with limited broadband options, a mobile hotspot is a practical bridge solution. T-Mobile's home internet product (unlimited, $50/month) works well in many O'ahu neighborhoods.

Pro Tip: Book Internet Installation Before You Arrive

This is the utility most commonly forgotten until move-in day. Installation appointments for Spectrum and Hawaiian Telcom typically have a 1–2 week lead time. Book your internet the same day you sign your lease.

Trash and Recycling on O'ahu

O'ahu trash and recycling collection is managed by the City and County of Honolulu's Department of Environmental Services. For single-family homes, curbside pickup is included in your property taxes — no separate billing required.

Collection Schedule

Collection days vary by neighborhood. Trash is picked up once per week; recycling is also weekly in most areas (blue bin). Large item/bulky item pickup can be scheduled through the city. Check your specific collection day at honoluludepartmentofenvironmentalservices.org or by calling 808-768-3200.

Condos and Apartments

Most condos and apartment buildings have internal trash chutes or dumpsters managed by the building. Individual unit owners don't schedule city pickup — it's handled by the HOA or building management.

Hawaii's Recycling Note

Hawaii has a container deposit program (HI-5): aluminum cans, glass bottles, and plastic bottles carry a $0.05 deposit per container, redeemable at redemption centers island-wide. Save your containers — returning them regularly adds up. Most grocery stores have HI-5 collection areas.

Solar on O'ahu — Worth Serious Consideration

Given HECO's electricity rates ($0.38–$0.45/kWh), solar power has among the fastest payback periods in the US on O'ahu. A properly sized system with battery storage can reduce your electricity bill by 60–80%, and the payback period for many O'ahu homes is 5–8 years.

If You're Buying

If you're purchasing a home, look for properties with existing solar installations — it's a genuine financial asset in Hawaii, not just a green feature. Systems with battery storage (Tesla Powerwall, Enphase IQ Battery) are particularly valuable for whole-home energy independence.

If you're buying a home without solar, get a quote from at least two installers before closing — it's worth factoring into your budget. Current installed cost for a residential system on O'ahu: $18,000–$35,000 before federal tax credits (30% federal ITC as of 2026).

If You're Renting

Ask whether the property has solar and whether the benefit is passed through to tenants (most Hawaii landlords retain HECO credits, but some pass through savings via reduced utility allowances). You can also look into community solar programs — you subscribe to a portion of a shared solar installation and receive credits on your HECO bill without roof installation.

HECO Grid Requirements

Any solar installation must be approved by HECO before connection. Processing times for grid interconnection applications run 30–90 days. This is a known frustration for new homeowners eager to go solar — budget the wait into your planning.

Military PCS — Utility Notes for On-Base and Off-Base

On-Base Housing (Privatized)

On-base housing on O'ahu (Hickam, Schofield, Marine Corps Base Hawaii) is managed by private companies under long-term leases. Utilities are typically included up to a monthly allowance (based on your BAH), with overage billed to you directly. Set up your account with the housing office on arrival — utilities are not set up through HECO directly for on-base residents.

Off-Base Housing

If you're renting or buying off-base, set up utilities exactly as described above. Most O'ahu landlords near military bases are experienced with PCS moves and can advise on local providers. HECO and BWS both have military-specific information and can accommodate early setups with your PCS orders as documentation in lieu of a deposit.

HECO Military Deposit Waiver

Active duty military members can often have the HECO deposit waived by providing a copy of their orders and military ID. Call 808-548-7311 and ask about the military deposit waiver program.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I set up electricity on O'ahu?

Contact Hawaiian Electric (HECO) at hawaiianelectric.com or 808-548-7311. Have your address, move-in date, SSN or ITIN, and photo ID ready. HECO can activate service within 1–2 business days. Set up at least 5 business days before move-in.

How do I set up water service on O'ahu?

Contact the Honolulu Board of Water Supply (BWS) at boardofwatersupply.com or 808-748-5000. You'll need your address, move-in date, and a security deposit ($50–$100). In most cases you're transferring billing — water is already flowing to the property.

Is there natural gas on O'ahu?

O'ahu does not have a natural gas distribution network in most areas. Hawaii Gas provides propane delivery and synthetic natural gas in limited Honolulu neighborhoods. Most homes use electric appliances. Check with your landlord or prior owner about what fuel your specific home uses.

What internet options are available on O'ahu?

The main providers are Spectrum (cable, widely available), Hawaiian Telcom (fiber/DSL, expanding footprint), and Starlink (satellite, island-wide). Book installation 3–4 weeks before move-in — Spectrum and Hawaiian Telcom typically have 1–2 week lead times.

How much are utilities on O'ahu per month?

A 3-bedroom home averages $315–$595/month in utilities (electricity $180–$350, water $45–$85, sewer $35–$60, internet $55–$100). Hawaii has among the highest electricity rates in the US ($0.38–$0.45/kWh). Solar can reduce electricity costs by 60–80%.
