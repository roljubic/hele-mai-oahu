Childcare on O'ahu is one of the most common sources of relocation shock for families moving from the mainland. The costs are higher than almost anywhere in the United States. The waitlists are real and long. And unlike most relocation logistics — finding movers, setting up utilities, registering a car — childcare can't be solved quickly after you arrive. It requires advance planning, and often more of it than families expect.

This guide is for parents of children under 6 who are relocating to O'ahu and need an honest picture of what the childcare landscape looks like: what it costs, where the options are, what subsidies exist, and what to do before your move date.

Cost Reality: What Childcare Actually Costs on O'ahu

Hawaii consistently ranks in the top 3–5 most expensive states for childcare in national surveys. O'ahu, as the most urbanized island with the highest concentration of working parents and limited licensed care supply, sits at the high end of Hawaii's already-elevated range.

Waitlists: The Most Underestimated Part

The waitlist situation on O'ahu is genuine and not something you can solve by being persistent or flexible. Supply is structurally constrained: licensed facilities have state-mandated staff-to-child ratios, physical space limits, and a labor market that makes staffing infant rooms particularly difficult. The most reputable centers in desirable neighborhoods frequently have infant room waitlists of 12–18 months at well-regarded programs.

What This Means in Practice

  • Start before you're pregnant if you can. This is not hyperbole — some Kailua and Manoa programs have waitlists long enough that families who added their names during pregnancy were offered spots when the child was 8–12 months old.
  • Apply to multiple programs simultaneously. There's no penalty for being on multiple waitlists. Apply broadly — 5–10 programs — across different neighborhoods and care types.
  • Waitlist fees are standard. Most programs charge a one-time or annual waitlist fee of $50–$200. This is normal. Pay it; it's how programs track serious applicants.
  • Give a realistic start date range. Don't commit to a specific start date on your waitlist application. Say "available anytime between [Month] and [Month + 3]" — this improves your odds of matching an opening.
  • Follow up quarterly. Programs lose waitlist contacts who don't follow up. A brief email every 2–3 months keeps you visible and updates them if your timeline changed.
The Arrival Gap Risk Families who don't arrange childcare before moving frequently face a gap — arriving on O'ahu with both parents ready to return to work and no childcare secured. Gaps of 3–6 months while waiting for a spot are reported regularly. Plan for this possibility: identify a backup nanny agency, research babysitting co-ops in your target neighborhood, or confirm that one parent has schedule flexibility in the first months after arrival.

Types of Childcare on O'ahu

Licensed Daycare Centers

Commercial facilities licensed by the Hawaii Department of Human Services (DHS). Multiple staff, structured curriculum, regulated ratios. Most common option for full-time care. Quality ranges significantly — DHS licensing sets a floor, not a ceiling. Ask about staff tenure (high turnover is a warning sign), staff-to-child ratios in each room, curriculum philosophy, and how communication with parents works.

Licensed Family Daycare Homes

DHS-licensed providers operating from a residential home, typically 4–8 children. Often less expensive than centers, smaller groups, more flexible hours. Quality is highly variable — the best family home providers build long waitlists of their own. The risk: a single provider means a single point of failure; if they're sick or have an emergency, you have no care that day. Always verify DHS license status.

In-Home Nanny

A nanny provides care in your home. Highest flexibility, most individualized care, no waitlist problem — but also the highest cost ($3,200–$4,800/month plus employment taxes), and you take on employer responsibilities: Hawaii payroll tax, workers' compensation, Prepaid Health Care Act coverage after 20 hours/week. Nanny agencies on O'ahu include Nannies of Honolulu and Hawaii Nanny Network. Sitter platforms (Care.com, Sittercity) can supplement but don't replace structured vetting.

Nanny Share

Two families sharing a nanny — each family pays a partial rate (typically $1,600–$2,400/month) for care that rotates between homes or stays at one home. Increasingly common in Hawaii Kai, Kailua, and Manoa among families who couldn't secure center spots. Finding a share partner requires networking in neighborhood Facebook groups or relocation communities.

Childcare Co-ops

Parent-operated cooperative childcare exists in some O'ahu communities, particularly on the Windward side (Kailua, Kaneohe). Usually requires parent volunteer hours in exchange for lower cost. Good option for parents with flexible schedules. Search "Oahu parent cooperative preschool" and local community Facebook groups.

Financial Assistance Programs

Child Care Subsidy Program (Hawaii DCCA/DHS)

Hawaii's federally-funded childcare subsidy pays a portion of licensed care costs for income-qualifying families on a sliding scale. For a family of 4 in 2026, income eligibility extends to approximately 85% of state median income — broader than many people assume. Apply through the Hawaii Department of Human Services: humanservices.hawaii.gov. Wait periods of 3–6 months are common; apply before you move if possible.

Pre-K Hawaii (Free Preschool for 4-Year-Olds)

The state's Pre-K Hawaii program provides free, part-day preschool for 4-year-olds at public school sites and community partner locations. Not all neighborhoods have available slots; enrollment is competitive and often waitlisted. Apply through the Hawaii Department of Education (hawaiiprekregistration.com) — applications typically open in January for the following school year. Families relocating mid-year should check with DOE directly about mid-year placement options.

Head Start and Early Head Start

Federally-funded Head Start serves income-qualifying children 3–5; Early Head Start serves infants and toddlers under 3 and pregnant women. Both are free. Quality varies by program site. Hawaii Head Start programs are administered through various community agencies — Good Beginnings Alliance maintains a directory.

Dependent Care FSA

If your employer offers a Flexible Spending Account for dependent care, maximize it — the federal limit is $5,000/year in pre-tax contributions, saving $1,000–$2,000+ in taxes depending on your bracket. This doesn't reduce your cost but reduces the after-tax hit significantly.

Military Families: On-Base Resources

Families stationed at Joint Base Pearl Harbor-Hickam, Schofield Barracks, Marine Corps Base Hawaii (Kaneohe Bay), or Bellows AFS have access to on-installation Child Development Centers (CDCs) — typically the most cost-effective quality childcare option available on O'ahu.

What CDCs Offer

  • Rates based on Total Family Income (TFI) — typically $350–$900/month depending on income, significantly below civilian rates
  • Full-day care, structured curriculum, DOD-licensed facilities
  • Before/after school programs for older children
  • Priority placement for single-parent families and dual-military couples

The Waitlist Reality for CDCs

CDCs at JBPHH and MCB Hawaii are heavily subscribed. Add your child to the CDC waitlist as soon as you receive PCS orders — ideally 6–9 months before your report date. Register through Army OneSource (armyonesource.com) or MCCS (mccshawaii.com) depending on your branch. Families who arrive without a CDC spot frequently use off-base care for 3–9 months while waiting.

Preschool Options: Philosophy and Programs

Montessori Programs

O'ahu has a meaningful Montessori community with programs in Manoa, Kailua, and Central O'ahu. Quality Montessori schools typically accept children from age 2.5–3. Popular programs include Montessori School of Maui (Oahu campus), Island Montessori, and several smaller AMI/AMS-affiliated programs. Expect waitlists and tuition in the $1,200–$2,000/month range for full-day preschool.

Faith-Based Preschools

Many O'ahu churches operate high-quality preschool programs at below-market rates. These are often not well-marketed outside their communities — local neighborhood Facebook groups and parent networks are the best way to discover them. Membership is typically not required. Check churches in your target neighborhood directly.

University Lab Schools

The University of Hawaii's Child Development Center (Manoa campus) and Kapiolani Community College's Early Childhood Education program have lab school programs. Wait-listed and competitive, but excellent quality and often below-market tuition.

Language Immersion Preschools

Hawaii has strong Hawaiian language (ʻōlelo Hawaiʻi) immersion options at the preschool level through the Pūnana Leo o Honolulu program — a full-immersion Hawaiian language preschool. Japanese and Mandarin immersion options also exist in some areas. For families wanting to give their child a multilingual foundation, O'ahu has more options than most mainland markets.

Pre-Move Action Checklist

If you're relocating to O'ahu in the next 6 months, this is what to do now:

  • ☐ Determine your target neighborhood — childcare availability is heavily neighborhood-dependent; don't finalize housing before knowing your care options in that area
  • ☐ Research 8–12 licensed centers and family homes in your target neighborhood via the Hawaii DHS child care license registry (humanservices.hawaii.gov)
  • ☐ Email or call each program — ask about current waitlist length, your expected placement timeline, and their waitlist process
  • ☐ Submit waitlist applications with deposits to your top 5–6 choices
  • ☐ Apply for DHS childcare subsidy if income may qualify — processing takes 60–90 days minimum
  • ☐ Check Pre-K Hawaii enrollment if your child will be 4 during the upcoming school year
  • ☐ Military families: register on CDC waitlist immediately upon receiving PCS orders
  • ☐ Identify a backup plan — nanny agency, nanny share network, or trusted family — for the gap between arrival and care securing
  • ☐ Budget $1,800–$2,800/month per child under 3 in your relocation financial model

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does daycare cost on O'ahu?

Full-time infant care runs $1,800–$2,800/month at licensed centers. Toddler care: $1,500–$2,400/month. Preschool: $900–$2,200/month depending on full or part-day. O'ahu is consistently among the most expensive childcare markets in the US.

Are there waitlists for O'ahu daycares?

Yes — infant room waitlists of 6–18 months at popular centers are common. Apply to 5–10 programs before you move, not after. Families who wait until arrival frequently face a care gap of 3–6+ months.

Does Hawaii have subsidized childcare?

Yes. The Child Care Subsidy Program (DHS) helps income-qualifying families. Pre-K Hawaii offers free preschool for 4-year-olds. Head Start/Early Head Start serves low-income families from birth. Military families have on-base CDC access at significantly lower rates.

Which neighborhoods have the best childcare options?

Manoa/Kaimuki, Kailua, Mililani, and Ewa Beach/Kapolei have the strongest density of quality options. Hawaii Kai and Kaneohe are solid. North Shore and Ko Olina are limited — factor this into your neighborhood decision.

Can I apply to O'ahu daycares before I arrive?

Yes — and you should. Most programs accept remote waitlist applications with a deposit. Contact programs with your anticipated move date and start window. Hele Mai O'ahu can coordinate this outreach on your behalf as part of our relocation service.

What do military families need to know about O'ahu childcare?

Register on the CDC waitlist the moment you receive PCS orders. On-base CDCs are TFI-based and far less expensive than civilian care. Off-base options are the backup — plan for a possible wait of 3–9 months for a CDC spot.
